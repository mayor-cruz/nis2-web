What is NIS2 and Why It Matters

The European Union's NIS2 Directive came into force on 16 January 2023, establishing enhanced cybersecurity requirements for critical infrastructure including healthcare. Despite Brexit, UK healthcare organizations remain affected through extraterritorial reach, data processing relationships with EU entities, and the UK's development of equivalent regulations to maintain alignment.

NIS2 Directive Overview

NIS2 Core Provisions for Healthcare

  • Expanded scope: Covers medium and large entities, including NHS Trusts and major providers
  • Enhanced risk management (Article 21): Risk analysis, incident handling, business continuity, supply chain security, secure acquisition, development, maintenance, cyber hygiene, cryptography, HR security, access control policies
  • Stricter reporting (Article 23): Significant incident notification within 24 hours, follow-up report in 72 hours
  • Senior management accountability (Article 20): Personal liability for non-compliance
  • Substantial penalties: Up to €10 million or 2% of turnover

UK Impact Despite Brexit

  • Processing EU citizen data or providing services to EU entities
  • Upcoming UK "enhanced NIS" regulations
  • Cross-border data-sharing frameworks
  • NHS service for EU citizens and data partnerships
Why This Matters:

Healthcare's digital transformation has created immense opportunities for improving patient care, operational efficiency, and data-driven decision-making. However, this evolution has concurrently heightened the exposure of healthcare systems to cyber threats. Recent incidents highlight the sector's vulnerability, including the 2020 University Hospital Düsseldorf ransomware attack linked to a patient death and the 2021 HSE (Ireland) ransomware attack compromising 520,000 people's data at a cost of €100 million.

The Growing Threat:

Imagine this: A hacker targets a small software company that provides appointment scheduling for your local GP. Suddenly, patient records become inaccessible, appointments are cancelled, and chaos ensues. This isn't a far-fetched scenario.

The UK healthcare sector's shift to digital through electronic health records, telemedicine, interconnected devices and other systems offers clear operational and patient-care benefits but also expands its cyber-attack surface (Rice 2025). As cybercriminals increasingly target healthcare, robust security is essential to protect patient safety, maintain data confidentiality and integrity, and ensure uninterrupted services.

Defining Supply Chain Vulnerabilities in UK Healthcare

In the context of UK healthcare, the supply chain refers to the end-to-end system that delivers everything a health service needs from the manufacturers of drugs, devices, and consumables through national and regional distribution networks, right to hospitals, GP practices, and, ultimately, patients. In modern healthcare, no organization operates in isolation (Alzahrani et al. 2022).

A vulnerability in this context is any weakness in people, processes, or technology that could be exploited accidentally or maliciously to compromise patient safety, data confidentiality, integrity, or system availability.

Did You Know?

The average NHS Trust works with hundreds of different suppliers, from medical device manufacturers and software providers to cleaning services and administrative support. Each of these connections is like another door - if even one door has a weak lock, it could be an entry point for cybercriminals.

Common Supply Chain Vulnerabilities

Lack of Visibility

Many healthcare organizations lack comprehensive insight into the cybersecurity practices and overall security posture of their numerous third-party vendors, making identifying and mitigating potential risks difficult.

CISSP Domain: Security and Risk Management, Security Operations

Insufficient Security Measures by Vendors

Third-party vendors, particularly smaller entities or those not primarily focused on cybersecurity, may have inadequate security controls, creating potential entry points for attackers (McBride 2024).

CISSP Domain: Software Development Security, Security and Risk Management

Inadequate Security Assessments

Healthcare organizations may not always conduct thorough and regular security assessments of their third-party vendors, potentially overlooking significant security weaknesses (High 2025).

CISSP Domain: Security Assessment and Testing

Lack of Contractual Security Requirements

Contracts with third-party vendors may not always include sufficiently detailed and stringent cybersecurity requirements, leading to ambiguity in security responsibilities (NHS Digital, 2023).

CISSP Domain: Security Architecture and Engineering

Poor Data Management by Vendors

Third-party vendors handling sensitive patient data may not always adhere to robust data management practices, increasing the risk of data breaches.

CISSP Domain: Asset Security, Security and Risk Management

Excessive Access Rights Granted to Vendors

Healthcare organizations might inadvertently grant overly broad access rights to their internal systems and sensitive data to third-party vendors, expanding the potential for misuse or compromise (McBride 2024).

CISSP Domain: Identity and Access Management (IAM)

From Weakness to Exploitation: Attack Vectors

Cybercriminals actively seek to exploit vulnerabilities within the healthcare supply chain through various attack vectors:

Malware Insertion

Attackers can inject malicious software into a vendor's system, which can then spread to the healthcare organization's network through updates, shared systems, or compromised devices. This exploits weaknesses in CISSP Domain 3 (Security Architecture and Engineering) related to secure system design and implementation (Alzahrani et al. 2022).

Phishing and Social Engineering

Attackers may target employees of third-party vendors with phishing emails or social engineering tactics to gain access to their credentials and then pivot to the healthcare organization's systems. This can bypass controls in CISSP Domain 5 (Identity and Access Management) (He et al. 2021).

Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)

Sophisticated attackers may establish a long-term presence in a vendor's network to gather intelligence and eventually launch a targeted attack on the healthcare organization. This often involves lateral movement across networks, relating to CISSP Domain 4 (Communication and Network Security) (He et al. 2021).

Compromised Software Updates

Attackers can tamper with software updates provided by vendors, embedding malicious code that is then distributed to the healthcare organization. This relates to CISSP Domain 3 (Security Architecture and Engineering) CISSP Domain 8 (Software Development Security).

Hardware Tampering

In some cases, attackers might compromise hardware components within the supply chain, introducing vulnerabilities at a fundamental level. This also falls under CISSP Domain 3 (Security Architecture and Engineering) (Schnackenburg 2025).

The Attacker's Toolkit: Technologies and Practices Used in Supply Chain Attacks

Cybercriminals utilize a diverse range of tools and techniques in supply chain attacks targeting healthcare (Traynor 2025):

Ransomware

Encrypting critical data and demanding a ransom for its release.

Phishing

Deceptive emails or messages to trick users into revealing sensitive information or clicking malicious links.

Supply Chain Phishing

Targeting trusted vendors to launch attacks throughout the supply chain.

SQL Injection

Exploiting vulnerabilities in databases.

Business Email Compromise (BEC)

Using spoofed emails to trick employees into making fraudulent money transfers.

Zero-Day Exploits

Attacking previously unknown vulnerabilities.

Hardware/Software Tampering

Inserting malicious components or code during manufacturing or distribution.

DDoS Attacks

Overwhelming systems with traffic to disrupt services.

Real-World Examples of Supply Chain Attacks

SolarWinds Attack (2020)

In late 2020, attackers carried out a supply-chain compromise of SolarWinds' Orion IT monitoring platform by inserting malicious code (known as SUNBURST) into routine software updates released between March and June 2020. Once deployed, the backdoor granted the adversary stealthy, high-privilege access to customer networks—roughly 18,000 organizations worldwide, including government agencies and critical infrastructure operators (Michael 2023).

WannaCry NHS Attack (2017)

In May 2017, the WannaCry ransomware, leveraging the NSA's "EternalBlue" exploit, infected over 200,000 devices worldwide and struck 34 NHS hospital trusts (about 34% of all trusts in England), locking staff out of PCs, MRI scanners, and other critical systems. The damage was worsened by vulnerable third-party software—many trusts were still running unsupported Windows XP and other unpatched systems supplied by external vendors, leaving them unable to receive crucial security updates (Ghafur et al. 2019).

Medical Device Vulnerabilities (2025)

The FDA has issued alerts about cybersecurity flaws in Contec CMS8000 and Epsimed MN-120 patient monitors that could allow unauthorized remote control or malfunction. Healthcare providers, facilities, patients and caregivers are being warned of these risks (Dixon 2025).

Critical Analysis of Existing Supply Chain Security Solutions

Current Landscape

UK healthcare organizations are using various approaches to defend against supply chain threats (Papaphilippou et al. 2023):

  • Security Frameworks: Adoption of frameworks like ISO 27001, NIST Cybersecurity Framework, and Cyber Essentials.
  • Asset Security: Regular security audits and penetration tests on systems and vendors.
  • Security Assessment: Conducting security audits and penetration testing of internal systems and third-party vendors.
  • Security Operations: Developing and testing incident response plans for cybersecurity incidents, including supply chain attacks.

Strengths and Weaknesses

While existing solutions provide a foundation, significant gaps remain:

Strengths
  • Frameworks like Cyber Essentials provide a starting point
  • Vendor risk management programs help identify risks
  • Growing awareness of supply chain vulnerabilities
  • Established incident response procedures
Weaknesses
  • Lack of visibility into entire supply chain
  • Inconsistent enforcement of security standards
  • Reliance on self-assessment and infrequent audits
  • Increasing complexity of supply chains
  • Limited resources for continuous monitoring

Room for Improvement in Supply Chain Security

Key areas for enhancing supply chain security include:

Enhanced Visibility

Implementing solutions that provide real-time visibility into the security posture of all vendors within the supply chain.

Continuous Monitoring

Shifting from periodic assessments to continuous monitoring of vendor security controls and potential threats by Zhang and Thompson (2023).

Stronger Contractual Agreements

Including specific and enforceable cybersecurity requirements in contracts with all vendors.

Collaboration and Information Sharing

Enhancing collaboration and sharing of threat intelligence among healthcare organizations and their suppliers (Johnson et al. 2023).

Leveraging Emerging Technologies

Exploring the use of AI and blockchain for enhanced security and supply chain integrity.

Zero Trust Architecture

Adopting a security model that assumes no implicit trust and requires continuous verification.

Supply Chain Security Assurance Framework (SCSAF)

The Supply Chain Security Assurance Framework (SCSAF) adopts a risk-based approach aligned with both NIS2 requirements and CISSP domains. It provides healthcare organizations with practical steps to identify, assess, and mitigate supply chain vulnerabilities:

Figure 1: NIS2 Compliance Roadmap for Supply Chain Management

Step 1: Map Your Supply Chain

This initial step creates a comprehensive inventory of all external entities your healthcare organization relies upon. Without a clear understanding of your supply chain, you'll have blind spots that are prime targets for attackers. This mapping helps visualize the flow of data, services, and goods and identify critical pathways.

Focus particularly on these critical vendor categories:

  • Medical device manufacturers and firmware providers: Crucial due to direct impact on patient care and safety. Compromised devices or malicious firmware updates can have life-threatening consequences.
  • Cloud service providers with access to patient data: Many organizations store and process patient information in the cloud. Their security is paramount to maintaining data confidentiality and compliance.
  • Software vendors supplying clinical applications: These are integral to daily operations, from electronic health records to appointment scheduling. If compromised, they can disrupt services and expose patient data.
  • Data processors handling sensitive information: Includes any third party processing patient data, such as analytics firms or transcription services. Their security practices directly impact your organization's data security.
Step 2: Categorize Vendors by Risk

Risk categorization allows you to prioritize security efforts and focus stringent controls on vendors posing the highest risk. This ensures you're not spreading resources too thin.

Evaluate using standardized risk criteria:

  • Criticality to clinical operations: How essential is this vendor to delivering patient care? A compromise of a highly critical vendor could lead to immediate and severe disruptions.
  • Level of access to sensitive data: How much access does the vendor have to patient health information or other sensitive data? Vendors with extensive access pose higher risk of data breaches.
  • Security maturity of the supplier: What is the vendor's own cybersecurity posture? A vendor with weak security is an easier target for attackers, who could then pivot to your systems.
  • Complexity of the supply relationship: How integrated is the vendor's system with your own? More complex integrations can create more potential attack pathways.
Step 3: Conduct Security Assessments

For vendors identified as high-risk, conduct thorough security assessments beyond simply asking about their security practices. These provide an objective evaluation of security controls and identify potential weaknesses.

Types of assessments include:

  • Questionnaires and documentation reviews to gather information about policies, procedures, and technical controls
  • Security audits: Independent reviews of security practices against established standards
  • Penetration testing: Simulated cyberattacks to identify vulnerabilities
Step 4: Review and Update Contracts

Contracts provide formal agreement on security responsibilities. Clearly defined security clauses ensure vendors are legally obligated to meet security standards and provide accountability for incidents.

For Medical Device Firmware:

  • Require cryptographically signed firmware updates to ensure integrity
  • Implement network segmentation for medical devices to limit breach impact
  • Deploy medical device security monitoring solutions
  • Establish firmware validation protocols

For Third-Party Data Processors:

  • Implement vendor privileged access management to control access rights
  • Establish data processing agreements with specific security requirements
  • Deploy continuous third-party security monitoring
  • Conduct regular security assessments
Step 5: Implement Continuous Monitoring

The threat landscape is constantly evolving, and a vendor's security posture can change over time. Continuous monitoring helps detect new vulnerabilities or security incidents that arise after initial assessment.

Methods include:

  • Security information and event management (SIEM) systems to monitor security logs
  • Threat intelligence feeds to stay informed about emerging threats
  • Regular communication with vendors to maintain open dialogue about security
  • Periodic reassessments to verify ongoing compliance
SCSAF Effectiveness:

Research by the National Cyber Security Centre (2023) shows that organizations implementing similar frameworks experience 43% fewer supply chain-related incidents compared to those without structured approaches.

Final Recommendation:

The bottom line is prioritising supply chain security is no longer optional—it is a fundamental imperative for protecting the operational integrity of the UK healthcare system, safeguarding sensitive patient data, and ultimately ensuring the safe and effective delivery of care.

Understanding Insider Threats in Healthcare

According to Verizon's Data breach investigation report 2023, "74% of all breaches include the human element, with people being involved either via Error, Privilege Misuse, Use of stolen credentials or Social Engineering (Information Commissioner's Office 2023).

Insider threats refer to risks posed by individuals with legitimate access to an organization's systems, data, or physical facilities who misuse their privileges either intentionally or accidentally. In healthcare, these insiders include staff, contractors, temporary workers, and business associates with varying levels of access to sensitive patient information and critical systems.

Critical Context:

Healthcare organizations are particularly vulnerable to insider threats due to the vast amount of sensitive data they handle, the large number of staff with varying access levels, and the critical nature of healthcare operations where delays or disruptions can directly impact patient care and safety.

Types of Insider Threats in Healthcare

Malicious Insiders

Employees or contractors who deliberately misuse their access privileges for personal gain, revenge, or other harmful purposes. This could include stealing patient data for sale on the dark web, sabotaging systems, or leaking confidential information.

CISSP Domain: Security and Risk Management, Identity and Access Management

Negligent Insiders

Staff members who unintentionally put the organization at risk through carelessness, lack of awareness, or failure to follow security protocols. Common examples include sharing passwords, falling for phishing attacks, or leaving sensitive information unattended.

CISSP Domain: Security and Risk Management, Identity and Access Management

Compromised Insiders

Legitimate users whose credentials or access have been compromised by external attackers who then use this access to perform malicious activities while appearing as a legitimate insider.

CISSP Domain: Security and Risk Management, Identity and Access Management

Vulnerabilities relating to Insider Threats in Healthcare

Excessive User Privileges

Employees may have more access than necessary, enabling unauthorized data access or manipulation.

CISSP Domain: Security and Risk Management, Identity and Access Management

Lack of Monitoring and Audit Logs

Absence of proper logging or SIEM monitoring makes it difficult to detect suspicious internal activities

CISSP Domain: Security and Risk Management,Security Operations

Weak Access Controls

Inadequate role-based access control (RBAC) or absence of multi-factor authentication increases the risk of misuse.

CISSP Domain: Security and Risk Management, Identity and Access Management

Poor Security Awareness and Training

Employees are unaware of acceptable behavior or how to report suspicious activity.

CISSP Domain: Security and Risk Management

The Attacker's Toolkit: Tactics and Practices Used in Insider Threat Attacks

Data Snooping

Healthcare staff accessing patient records without legitimate need, often out of curiosity about celebrities, colleagues, friends, or family members.

Data Theft

Deliberate extraction of patient data or intellectual property for financial gain, often to sell on the dark web or to competitors.

Sabotage

Disgruntled employees deliberately disrupting systems or services to harm the organization, potentially impacting patient care.

Unintentional Exposure

Staff accidentally exposing sensitive data through improper handling, such as sending emails to wrong recipients or misconfiguring public access to databases.

Shadow IT

Use of unauthorized software, applications, or devices that bypass security controls and potentially create new vulnerabilities.

Case Studies & Examples

Former NHS secretary found guilty of illegally accessing medical records (Information Commissioner's Office 2023)

A former NHS employee has been found guilty and fined for illegally accessing the medical records of over 150 people. It was further discovered that she had accessed a total of 156 patient records without consent or a business need, viewing them over 1800 times within the three-month period. This included the records of family members and individuals with postcodes local to where she lived at the time

Former NHS healthcare worker access health records without a valid business reason (DAC Beachcroft 2022)

A former NHS healthcare worker, was prosecuted for unlawfully accessing the medical records of 14 patients at South Warwickshire NHS Foundation Trust between June and December 2019 without a valid business reason or the Trust's knowledge. He personally knew the patients and accessed their sensitive data in violation of Section 170(1) of the Data Protection Act 2018. His actions led to a criminal compensation order of £3,000, with the ICO highlighting the breach as a serious violation of patient trust and privacy

Medico Inc Data Breach (UpGuard Team 2019)

On June 20, 2019, UpGuard's Data Breach Research Team discovered an exposed Amazon S3 bucket named "medicoar," containing potentially sensitive healthcare-related files linked to Medico Inc., a medical billing and consulting provider. The exposed data, consistent with Medico's services in billing, transcription, and compliance audits, included information that could be exploited if accessed by malicious actors. Upon being notified on June 21, Medico acted swiftly to secure the bucket within hours, mitigating further risk. This incident highlights the importance of securing cloud storage configurations, particularly for organisations handling sensitive healthcare data. This incident demonstrates a negligent insider who misconfigured critical services in the organisations.

Detection & Monitoring Strategies

Effective detection of insider threats requires a multi-layered approach using both technical controls and behavioral monitoring:

User Activity Monitoring

Implementing solutions that track user activities across systems, particularly focusing on access to sensitive data or privileged operations. This includes monitoring database queries, file access, and system configuration changes.

Behavioral Analytics

Using AI and machine learning to establish baseline behavior patterns for users and identify anomalies that may indicate malicious activity, such as unusual access times, excessive data downloads, or access to resources outside of normal job functions.

Data Loss Prevention (DLP)

Deploying tools that monitor and control the transfer of sensitive data, detecting unauthorized attempts to copy, forward, or exfiltrate protected information through email, removable media, or cloud services.

Access Reviews

Conducting regular reviews of user access rights to ensure that privileges align with current job responsibilities and removing excessive permissions that could be exploited.

Mitigation Strategies

Preventing and minimizing the impact of insider threats requires a comprehensive approach:

Technical Controls
  • Least Privilege Access: Granting users only the minimum access needed for their role
  • Privileged Access Management: Special controls for high-level access
  • Multi-factor Authentication: Requiring multiple verification methods
  • Network Segmentation: Limiting lateral movement within networks
  • Encryption: Protecting sensitive data even if accessed
Administrative Controls
  • Security Awareness Training: Educating staff about risks and responsibilities
  • Background Checks: Vetting employees before granting access
  • Clear Policies: Establishing explicit rules for data handling
  • Separation of Duties: Requiring multiple people for critical actions
  • Exit Procedures: Prompt removal of access when staff leave

Insider Threat Management Framework (ITMF)

The Insider Threat Management Framework provides healthcare organizations with a structured approach to address insider risks:

Figure 2: NIS2 Insider Threat Compliance Checklist

Phase 1: Program Foundation
  • Establish an insider threat working group with representatives from IT, HR, legal, and clinical departments
  • Develop comprehensive policies and procedures for handling insider threats
  • Define roles and responsibilities for insider threat management
  • Secure executive sponsorship and resource commitment
Phase 2: Risk Assessment
  • Identify critical assets and sensitive data that require protection
  • Assess vulnerabilities in current systems and processes
  • Evaluate existing access controls and identify excessive privileges
  • Map potential insider threat scenarios and their impact
Phase 3: Implementation
  • Deploy technical controls for monitoring and prevention
  • Implement least privilege access model across systems
  • Establish baseline behavior patterns for users and systems
  • Develop automated alerting for suspicious activities
  • Conduct regular security awareness training
Phase 4: Response Planning
  • Create incident response procedures specific to insider threats
  • Define escalation pathways and decision-making authority
  • Establish coordination with HR, legal, and communications teams
  • Prepare evidence collection and preservation procedures
Phase 5: Continuous Improvement
  • Conduct regular reviews of the insider threat program
  • Analyze incidents and near-misses for lessons learned
  • Update controls and procedures based on emerging threats
  • Measure and report on program effectiveness

Cybersecurity Solutions for NIS2 Compliance

Effective cybersecurity solutions for UK healthcare organizations must address both supply chain vulnerabilities and insider threats while aligning with NIS2 requirements.

Integrated Security Approach

Zero Trust Architecture

Implementing a security model that assumes no implicit trust and requires continuous verification of all users, devices, and resources, regardless of their location relative to the network perimeter.

Security Orchestration, Automation and Response (SOAR)

Deploying platforms that integrate security tools and automate incident response to improve detection and remediation of threats from both internal and external sources.

Privileged Access Management

Implementing comprehensive solutions for controlling, monitoring, and securing privileged access across the organization and its supply chain partners.

Continuous Monitoring

Establishing capabilities for real-time visibility into security posture, user behavior, and system activities to quickly identify potential threats or vulnerabilities.

NIS2-Specific Controls

Incident Reporting Framework

Developing systems and procedures for rapid notification of significant incidents within the 24-hour timeline required by NIS2.

Supply Chain Risk Management

Implementing comprehensive processes for assessing, monitoring, and mitigating security risks throughout the supply chain.

Security Governance Structure

Establishing clear roles, responsibilities, and accountability mechanisms for cybersecurity management to address senior management liability requirements.

Regular Security Testing

Conducting penetration tests, vulnerability assessments, and security audits to identify and address weaknesses before they can be exploited.

Comprehensive Recommendations

Based on the analysis of cybersecurity risks in UK healthcare under NIS2, we recommend a structured approach that addresses both supply chain vulnerabilities and insider threats:

1. Conduct a Comprehensive Risk Assessment

Start with a thorough evaluation of your organization's current security posture, focusing on:

  • Mapping all supplier relationships and their access to systems and data
  • Identifying all privileged users and evaluating access rights
  • Assessing existing security controls against NIS2 requirements
  • Prioritizing gaps based on potential impact to patient safety and data security
2. Implement the Supply Chain Security Assurance Framework (SCSAF)

Follow the five-step process outlined in the SCSAF to systematically address supply chain risks:

  • Map your supply chain with special attention to critical vendors
  • Categorize vendors by risk using standardized criteria
  • Conduct thorough security assessments of high-risk vendors
  • Review and update contracts to include robust security requirements
  • Implement continuous monitoring of vendor security posture
3. Deploy the Insider Threat Management Framework (ITMF)

Address internal risks through the five-phase approach:

  • Establish program foundation with clear policies and responsibilities
  • Conduct risk assessment to identify critical assets and vulnerabilities
  • Implement technical and administrative controls
  • Develop incident response plans specific to insider threats
  • Continuously improve based on performance metrics and emerging threats
4. Prioritize Technology Investments

Focus technology investments on solutions that address both supply chain and insider risks:

  • Privileged Access Management (PAM) systems
  • User and Entity Behavior Analytics (UEBA) platforms
  • Vendor Risk Management (VRM) solutions
  • Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) systems
  • Data Loss Prevention (DLP) tools
5. Foster a Security-Conscious Culture

Develop a strong security culture throughout the organization:

  • Implement comprehensive security awareness training for all staff
  • Establish clear security expectations for vendors and partners
  • Create channels for reporting security concerns without fear of reprisal
  • Recognize and reward security-conscious behaviors
  • Ensure leadership visibly champions security initiatives
Final Recommendation:

Prioritizing supply chain security and insider threat management is no longer optional—it's a fundamental imperative for protecting the operational integrity of UK healthcare systems, safeguarding sensitive patient data, and ultimately ensuring the safe and effective delivery of care. By implementing these frameworks and recommendations, healthcare organizations can significantly reduce their exposure to these critical risks and align with the enhanced requirements of NIS2.
